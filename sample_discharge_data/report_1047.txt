Reason for Admission:
	Timothy Alexander was admitted to the hospital due to a series of symptoms, including abdominal pain, weight loss, and changes in bowel habits persisting over several weeks. On admission, his vital signs were stable, but his abdomen appeared distended, and he exhibited signs of anemia. Initial laboratory results indicated an elevated carcinoembryonic antigen (CEA) level, confirming a suspicion of malignancy. Further diagnostic investigations, including computed tomography (CT) scans and colonoscopy, revealed a large tumor in the right colon. The tumor had invaded the surrounding tissues and had metastasized to several lymph nodes, indicating Stage III colorectal cancer.

Medical History:
	Timothy Alexander has a history of hypertension, diabetes mellitus type 2, and chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD), for which he was taking multiple medications prior to admission. He underwent a partial colectomy eight years ago due to diverticulitis. There is no significant family history of cancer.

Diagnostic Findings:
	Pathology results confirmed the diagnosis of adenocarcinoma of the colon. Imaging studies revealed multiple liver metastases, which were consistent with the elevated CEA levels. Blood tests showed anemia and abnormal liver function tests, indicating metastatic involvement.

Treatment Plan:
	A multidisciplinary team developed a comprehensive treatment plan for Timothy Alexander. He underwent a right hemicolectomy with lymph node dissection followed by a permanent ileostomy due to the tumor's location. Postoperative care involved close monitoring of his recovery, management of pain, and prevention of complications such as sepsis. A six-month course of adjuvant chemotherapy was initiated, consisting of FOLFOX (folinic acid, fluorouracil, oxaliplatin) and bevacizumab to target the metastatic lesions in his liver. The treatment plan also included radiation therapy for the liver metastases to reduce their size and minimize symptoms.

Hospital Course:
	Timothy Alexander's recovery from surgery was uneventful, with a gradual improvement in his bowel function. He faced several challenges during his hospital stay, including anemia, electrolyte imbalances, and fluid overload due to ileus and ascites. These were managed through intravenous iron supplementation, electrolyte replacement, and diuretics, respectively. His diabetes required close monitoring throughout the treatment process.
